# Dr. John Watson

## A Chronicle of London in Disarray: Notes from a Bystanding Physician

### Chapter One: When the City Stops Breathing

I have always believed that a city is not so different from a human body.

Roads are its veins, traffic its bloodstream.

Electricity and signals form its nervous system.

And certain landmarks—Big Ben among them—serve as its heartbeat.

Not because they control time, but because they remind everyone that time is still moving.

So when I stood outside the police cordon and looked up at the motionless clock face, a deep medical unease settled in my chest.

Not panic.

Something closer to oxygen deprivation.

Time is not meant to stop like that.

The police had sealed off the area. People clustered at the edges of the streets, cameras raised, voices low and uncertain. Some took photographs, others whispered theories, and many simply stood there, as if the rhythm of the city had been interrupted and they no longer knew how to move.

I did not look up immediately.

I have always made it a habit to look at the ground first.

In moments of chaos, the ground tells the truth.

Footprints reveal direction and urgency. Dropped objects speak of haste. Trampled paper shows where attention faltered. I walked slowly along the perimeter until something near the curb caught my eye.

A folded piece of paper.

Not a newspaper, not a leaflet—but a transport route map, worn smooth at the edges. It had been folded and unfolded countless times, the kind of wear that only comes from long-term use.

I picked it up.

The markings on it made me pause. These were not the notes of a tourist. Certain turns had been reinforced with heavy strokes; specific stretches of road were emphasized in red. This was the work of someone who knew the city well enough to optimize their escape.

As I studied it, something else drew my attention.

Scattered across the pavement were tiny flecks of color.

Red.

Not brick dust, but the unmistakable texture of chipped paint. I nudged one fragment gently with the toe of my shoe. Against the gray stone, the color was jarring.

I did not draw conclusions yet.

A physician should never rush a diagnosis.

I folded the map carefully and slipped it into my coat pocket.

But I knew this: the paper and the red fragments would eventually lead to the same answer.

### Chapter Two: A Bus That Was Never on the Schedule

By the time we reached Westminster Bridge, the morning fog had lifted.

The wind off the Thames carried a sharp chill. Commuters gathered near the bus stop, but their attention was fixed on something that did not belong.

A red double-decker bus.

It stood there idling, yet felt wrong in subtle ways. No route number. No familiar destination names. The electronic display flickered with distorted symbols, as if language itself had been scrambled.

I observed it from a distance.

The shade of red on the bodywork matched almost exactly the paint fragments I had seen earlier. Not fresh paint, but the dulled hue of a vehicle that had endured years of weather and use.

I moved closer.

Near the bench at the stop, partially hidden from casual view, something reflected the light. A small rectangle of plastic lay on the ground.

An Oyster card.

I picked it up.

The card itself was unremarkable, its edges worn smooth from years of handling. But when I turned it over, I noticed a name written on the back in pen. The ink had faded, yet the handwriting remained steady—precise, deliberate.

This was not idle graffiti.

It was the mark of someone accustomed to recording details.

I closed my fingers around the card, feeling a weight that had nothing to do with mass. It felt like a deliberate breadcrumb, left behind for someone who would recognize its significance.

From within the bus came a low mechanical hum, as though some system was recalculating.

I did not look at the doors.

I simply memorized the name and slipped the card into my pocket.

### Chapter Three: Stones Do Not Lie

The British Museum has always inspired reverence in me.

Not because of its scale, but because it houses humanity’s attempts to understand itself. Scripts, carvings, fragments of meaning—our collective resistance to being forgotten.

The sound of alarms shattered that quiet reverence.

We entered through a passage not meant for the public. Inside the Egyptian gallery, the lights flickered. The Rosetta Stone was encased within a shimmering energy field, pale blue light crawling slowly across its surface.

I stepped closer.

Most of the inscriptions lay dormant, ancient and silent. But one symbol was different.

It glowed faintly.

Not the entire figure—just a slender, sinuous section, as though something had recently disturbed it. The shape was unmistakable, coiled and alert.

I did not touch it.

I simply fixed its position in my memory.

Then I looked down.

Near the display case, partially crushed underfoot, lay a scrap of paper. I retrieved it and smoothed it out.

A receipt.

There was only one item listed. Not a souvenir. Not an academic volume. It was a utilitarian guide—dense, practical, and invaluable to a very specific kind of professional.

I recognized the type immediately.

I exhaled softly.

This individual was always planning the next movement.

Even in the middle of a crime, preparation never ceased.

I folded the receipt and placed it with the Oyster card.

The fragments were beginning to align.

### Chapter Four: A City on Wheels

Outside the museum, London had seized up completely.

Rush hour traffic resembled a chronic illness—slow, persistent, and capable of undermining any plan. After several attempts, we finally flagged down a black cab.

The driver did not open the door right away.

His eyes were sharp, experienced—the gaze of someone who had spent decades navigating the city’s arteries. I did not explain our situation. Instead, I spoke with him about the roads.

Where traffic was thickest.

Which routes had been diverted.

Which streets were, for all practical purposes, unusable today.

When he mentioned one particular road, his tone changed. Construction, barriers, temporary signals—everything about it signaled wasted time. For him, that street simply did not exist today.

I committed the name to memory.

Some answers do not need to be forced.

They only need to be heard.

By the time the door unlocked, I already knew which route must be avoided at all costs.

### Chapter Five: Poets and Keys

The air inside Westminster Abbey was heavy.

The Coronation Chair stood illuminated, ancient and imposing. Staff moved quietly around it, focused on their tasks, unaware of the significance beneath their feet.

I crouched to examine the base.

The carvings were intricate, each mythical beast steeped in symbolism. But behind one section, the stonework felt subtly wrong. The gap was too deliberate, too clean.

The compartment was locked.

I did not attempt to force it. Instead, my thoughts returned to the scattered notes, the layered symbolism, and the suspect’s fondness for history.

If a key had been hidden, it would not be placed arbitrarily.

It would rest somewhere protected by time itself.

My mind turned to Poets’ Corner. Weathered tombs. Worn stone slabs. And the earliest of them all—the one whose presence had shaped that sacred space.

I drew a steady breath and headed in that direction.

History does not speak.

But it leaves room for those who know how to listen.
