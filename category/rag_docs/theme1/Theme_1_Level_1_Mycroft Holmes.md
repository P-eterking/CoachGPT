# Mycroft Holmes

## On the Proper Use of Authority

### Chapter I: Codes Are Never Decorative

It is a popular misconception that power is loud.

This belief is usually held by those who do not possess it.

True authority is quiet, procedural, and—above all—documented. It exists not in dramatic gestures, but in codes, signatures, and phrases that only function when spoken correctly and at the correct level.

When the alert regarding the Elizabeth Tower reached my desk, I did not react immediately. Not because I was unconcerned, but because concern is inefficient.

Instead, I opened the maintenance registry.

Every structure of national significance has one. Not a public document, of course, but a layered archive—engineering protocols nested inside ceremonial exemptions, cross-referenced with security contingencies.

The Tower’s file was thick.

I navigated past the expected sections: inspections, restorations, symbolic constraints. What interested me was not the clock’s condition, but the language required to authorize intervention.

Mechanical failure can be addressed by engineers.
Symbolic interruption requires something else.

Buried beneath a subsection marked Heritage Exceptions, I found the relevant entry. It was not poetic. It was not memorable. It was precisely the sort of string designed never to be spoken aloud unless absolutely necessary.

A code composed not for elegance, but for control.

I read it once.
That was sufficient.

In systems of governance, repetition is for subordinates.

### Chapter II: Authority Is a Sentence, Not a Suggestion

The bus presented a jurisdictional anomaly.

Vehicles that do not appear on official schedules technically do not exist. Which, paradoxically, makes them far more troublesome than those that do.

To requisition something nonexistent, one must not request access.

One must override absence.

I accessed the emergency transport directive archive—a rarely used subsystem reserved for wartime logistics, counterterrorism operations, and situations in which plausible deniability must be maintained.

The directive language was rigid.

No room for interpretation.
No allowance for curiosity.

The command phrase itself was inelegant, assembled from procedural fragments and security qualifiers. It had been designed to be spoken only by someone who already understood its consequences.

I authorized it.

Not aloud.
Not theatrically.

I entered it exactly as written, including the delimiters most people overlook.

Systems, like people, obey when addressed correctly.

### Chapter III: Museums Are Controlled Environments

The public believes museums are repositories of history.

They are not.

They are repositories of permission.

Every object on display exists there not because it is old, but because it has been approved—catalogued, classified, and indexed within a hierarchy invisible to the casual observer.

The fragment derived from the Rosetta Stone was no exception.

Its public designation was intentionally vague. Accessibility requires ambiguity. But beneath that surface label was a deeper classification—one used only for items with geopolitical sensitivity.

I accessed the restricted catalog.

The entry was concise. Cold. Entirely unconcerned with narrative or romance. A sequence of characters, prefixed and segmented according to departmental origin.

I noted it.

The number itself mattered less than its implications. Objects catalogued in this manner are not merely artifacts.

They are leverage.

### Chapter IV: Licenses as Instruments of Compliance

The driver was unremarkable.

Which, in London, is itself remarkable.

Black cab operators are among the most regulated professionals in the city. Their knowledge is legendary; their compliance, mandatory.

Every licensed driver exists within a web of registrations, renewals, and revocable permissions.

I accessed the transport authority database.

The individual in question was current. No violations. No suspensions. But every record leaves a trail, and trails always include identifiers.

The license number was present, formatted according to contemporary standards. Not old enough to be obscure. Not new enough to be provisional.

It was the kind of number that carries weight only when cited by someone who knows where to send it.

I committed it to memory.

Authority does not threaten.
It reminds.

### Chapter V: Names Are the Most Dangerous Things

Documents are rarely powerful because of what they say.

They are powerful because of what they are called.

The file in question had circulated through various departments under euphemisms—placeholders designed to obscure significance without attracting attention.

Those names were irrelevant.

What mattered was the official designation.

The one used in interdepartmental correspondence.
The one that appears only in secure headers and ministerial briefings.

I retrieved it from the archive.

The title was formal, unambiguous, and burdened with centuries of implication. A name that, if spoken publicly, would provoke speculation. If mishandled, destabilization.

I did not linger on it.

Names, like power, function best when handled briefly.

Epilogue: Order Is Not the Absence of Chaos

People often ask what my role is in moments like these.

They imagine coordination. Strategy. Oversight.

In truth, my function is simpler.

I ensure that when decisive action becomes necessary, the correct words already exist.

Not everyone is permitted to speak them.

But someone must remember them.

That, ultimately, is my responsibility.
